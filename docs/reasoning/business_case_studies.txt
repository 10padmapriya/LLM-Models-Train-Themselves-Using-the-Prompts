ML Business Case Studies and Lessons Learned
=============================================

Case Study 1: Netflix Recommendation Engine
Business impact: Netflix estimates its recommendation system saves over $1 billion
per year in customer retention by reducing churn.
Technical approach: Collaborative filtering (what similar users watched) combined
with content-based features (genre, actors, themes) and contextual signals
(time of day, device type, how the user found the title).
Key lessons:
- A 1% improvement in recommendation relevance at scale = massive revenue
- Measuring watch time and completion rate matters more than star ratings
- The thumbnail image shown influences clicks more than the algorithm in some cases
- Personalizing the homepage layout (not just titles) doubled engagement

Case Study 2: Amazon Demand Forecasting
Business impact: Reduced excess inventory costs by 30%, improved delivery speed,
reduced stockouts on fast-moving products.
Technical approach: Hierarchical time series forecasting across millions of SKUs.
Models account for seasonality, promotions, weather, and regional differences.
Amazon uses DeepAR, a probabilistic forecasting model built on recurrent neural networks.
Key lessons:
- Forecasting demand 6-8 weeks out allows smarter warehouse stocking decisions
- Probabilistic forecasts (give a range, not a point estimate) are more useful
  than point forecasts for inventory decisions
- The business process around the model matters as much as the model itself

Case Study 3: Fraud Detection at PayPal
Business impact: Reduced fraud losses by 50% while increasing approval rates
for legitimate transactions (reducing false positives).
Technical approach: Real-time scoring of every transaction within 300ms using
gradient boosting. Features include transaction amount, location, device fingerprint,
time patterns, and network graph of related accounts.
Key lessons:
- Error costs are asymmetric: missing fraud is very expensive, but so is
  blocking legitimate customers. Define acceptable tradeoffs explicitly.
- Fraudsters adapt to your model. Continuous retraining is mandatory.
- Graph features (is this account connected to known fraud accounts?) are
  among the most predictive features.

Case Study 4: Uber Surge Pricing
Business impact: Surge pricing increases driver supply during peak demand,
reducing wait times by 50% and increasing platform revenue.
Technical approach: Real-time supply/demand forecasting by geographic zone.
Gradient boosting predicts demand 5-15 minutes ahead. Price elasticity models
determine optimal multiplier.
Key lessons:
- ML-driven dynamic pricing requires careful communication to users to avoid backlash
- The model must be explainable enough for customer service to handle complaints
- Feedback loops matter: higher prices attract more drivers which reduces surge,
  which must be accounted for in the model

Common ML Project Failure Modes:
1. Solving the wrong problem
Building a model for a problem that doesn't need ML. A pricing analyst manually
adjusts prices and is 85% accurate. You build an ML model that is 87% accurate.
The 2% improvement doesn't justify the engineering cost and risk.
Always ask: what is the baseline, and is ML necessary to beat it?

2. Data leakage
Using information in training that would not be available at prediction time.
Example: predicting loan default using a credit bureau feature that is only
pulled after a default occurs. Model looks great in testing, collapses in production.
Prevention: strictly simulate the exact information available at prediction time.

3. No monitoring after deployment
Models degrade over time as the world changes. Customer behavior shifts,
product catalog changes, seasonality introduces new patterns.
Without monitoring you won't know until something goes badly wrong.
Every production model needs: input distribution monitoring, prediction distribution
monitoring, and business KPI tracking.

4. Ignoring deployment complexity
A model in a Jupyter notebook is not a product. Deployment requires an API,
input validation, logging, versioning, rollback capability, and documentation.
Data scientists who can't deploy their own models create bottlenecks.

5. Lack of stakeholder alignment
Model is built to optimize a metric that stakeholders don't care about.
Always define success metrics with stakeholders before modeling, not after.

How to Build the Business Case for an ML Project:
Step 1: Quantify the current cost of the problem
"We lose $2M per year to fraud that manual review misses."

Step 2: Estimate the improvement ML can achieve
"Based on similar problems at other companies, we expect 40-60% reduction."

Step 3: Calculate expected value
"Conservative estimate: $800K saved per year."

Step 4: Estimate project cost
"3 months of 2 data scientists + infrastructure = $300K."

Step 5: Calculate ROI
"Year 1 ROI: ($800K - $300K) / $300K = 167%. Payback in 4.5 months."

Step 6: Define the pilot
"We will run a 6-week pilot on 10% of transactions before full rollout."
