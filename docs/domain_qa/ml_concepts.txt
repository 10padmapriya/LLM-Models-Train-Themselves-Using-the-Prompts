Core Machine Learning Concepts
================================

Supervised vs Unsupervised Learning:
Supervised learning uses labeled examples to train a model.
The model learns to map inputs to outputs by minimizing prediction error.
Examples: classification (spam/not spam), regression (predict house price).

Unsupervised learning finds patterns in unlabeled data.
The model discovers structure on its own without being told the answer.
Examples: clustering customers by behavior, anomaly detection, topic modeling.

Overfitting and Underfitting:
Overfitting occurs when a model memorizes the training data including its noise,
and fails to generalize to new examples.
Signs: very high training accuracy, much lower test accuracy.
Solutions: more data, regularization (L1/L2), simpler model, dropout, early stopping.

Underfitting occurs when a model is too simple to capture the underlying pattern.
Signs: low accuracy on both training and test sets.
Solutions: more complex model, better features, less regularization, more training.

The Bias-Variance Tradeoff:
Bias: Error from wrong assumptions in the model. High bias = underfitting.
Variance: Error from sensitivity to small fluctuations in training data. High variance = overfitting.
Total Error = Bias^2 + Variance + Irreducible Noise
Goal: find the model complexity that minimizes total error.

Cross-Validation:
K-fold cross-validation splits data into K subsets (folds).
The model trains on K-1 folds and validates on the held-out fold, rotating K times.
Final score is the average across all K runs.
This gives a more reliable estimate of generalization than a single train/test split.
Common choice: K=5 or K=10.

Regularization Techniques:
L1 (Lasso): Adds the sum of absolute values of weights to the loss function.
Effect: Drives some weights to exactly zero, performing automatic feature selection.
Use when: You suspect many features are irrelevant.

L2 (Ridge): Adds the sum of squared weights to the loss function.
Effect: Shrinks all weights toward zero but rarely eliminates them completely.
Use when: All features may be relevant but you want to prevent large weights.

Gradient Descent:
The optimization algorithm that trains most ML models.
Steps:
1. Initialize weights randomly
2. Calculate loss (prediction error) on training data
3. Compute the gradient (direction of steepest increase in loss)
4. Update weights in the opposite direction: w = w - learning_rate * gradient
5. Repeat until loss converges

Learning rate too high: training is unstable, loss oscillates or diverges.
Learning rate too low: training is very slow, may get stuck in local minima.

Ensemble Methods:
Bagging (Bootstrap Aggregating): Train many models on random subsets of data,
average their predictions. Reduces variance. Example: Random Forest.

Boosting: Train models sequentially, each one correcting the errors of the previous.
Reduces bias. Examples: XGBoost, LightGBM, AdaBoost.

Stacking: Train a meta-model on the predictions of base models.
Often achieves the best accuracy but is complex to implement and interpret.

Feature Importance:
Understanding which features drive predictions is critical for business trust.
Methods:
- Permutation importance: shuffle a feature and measure accuracy drop
- SHAP values: game-theoretic approach, works for any model
- Tree-based importance: built into Random Forest and XGBoost
- Coefficient magnitude: for linear models
